How to use word list

Word List

This is a list of the Latin words used in the tutorial. It is set out so that you can
  • Find each word easily
  • Recognise whether it is a noun, verb, adjective etc.
  • Identify which declension it belongs to (if it’s a noun or adjective) or which conjugation it belongs to (if it’s a verb)
  • Know what to do next to decline it

By using our word list, you will gain practice and confidence with Latin.
When you complete the tutorial, you will have the confidence to use a Latin – English dictionary. We suggest R.E.
Latham, Revised Medieval Latin Word-list, (London, published for the British Academy by the Oxford University Press,
1973).
Other Latin dictionaries are set out in the same manner as our word list. However, you may find that they may provide less information.
Remember that if you look up a medieval, Tudor or Stuart word in a Classical Latin dictionary, it may give a different and inappropriate meaning.

How to use word list

Remember that this word list gives the most common medieval spelling of each word. It does not list the many variants
of the word. In particular, in Tudor and Stuart documents you may find that the word is spelt with an ‘ae’ instead of an
‘e’.

Nouns

Nouns are given in the nominative singular, followed by the genitive singular ending. The gender of the noun is given
and its meaning in English.
Look at our example of terra

If you know the genitive singular of a noun – you can work out which case it belongs to.
If you know which case it belongs to - you can work out how to decline it. The genitive singular of third declension nouns is particularly important. Therefore, both the nominative and genitive
singular of third declension nouns is given in full.

Verbs

Verbs are given in parts (called the principal parts).
Look at our example of habere to have

Remember what the first three parts tell you

  • Part one - the 'I' form of the present tense
  • Part two - the infinitive
  • Part three - the 'I' form of the past tense

With this information, you will be able to conjugate each verb.
Sometimes part four is given.

Adjectives

These are all noted. For first and second declension adjectives, the masculine, feminine and neuter nominative singular
forms are given, with their meaning.

Some first and second declension adjectives end ‘er’ in the masculine nominative singular. Most lose the ‘e’ of ‘er’ when
declined.

For third declension adjectives either the

  • Masculine/feminine and neuter nominative singular forms are given, with the meaning.

Or the

  • Nominative and genitive forms are given, with the meaning.

For an explanation of this, see lesson seven.

Pronouns

The masculine, feminine and neuter nominative singular forms are given, with their meaning. All pronouns are noted.
For example:

qui, qui, quod (pronoun) who, whom, which

Adverbs

These are all noted, with their meaning.
For example:

class="problem"> ubi

(adverb) where, when

Prepositions

These are all noted, with the case they take and their meaning.
For example:

ante (presposition + accusative) before

Abbreviations

These abbreviations are used in the word list:
For example

  • (f.)feminine
  • (m.)masculine
  • (n.)neuter

Brackets

Where necessary, any further explanation of a word is given in brackets.
For exampleS

probo, probare, probavi, probatum (1) to prove (a will)
